Issues

Though most of my pieces for the final design came together, the final product is definitely rough around the edges.

I would have preferred to use a 3D printed model for the dancer figure, but I could not make any models I found of the character work.
They were extracted from a game and designed for rigging, not 3D printing.

Also, the wiring, though not visible from the front with the lid closed, does stand out a lot.
I would like to revise this design in such a way that the wires are entirely hidden and the metal plates are much smaller.

The amount of wood glue I used seems excessive and detracts from the finished look.
It also managed to glue down some metal shavings from cutting the nails and screws.

That would have been a less problematic issue if the caddies I had designed for the compartments had worked out.

I was rather satisfied with the designs, but my sizing was a bit off and I did not manage to get them reprinted in the proper size.
Without these, the inside feels rather empty (though admittedly, it is just an empty box at the moment).
Scaling it to be smaller may also help with this problem.

I also like the hinges and latch, but attaching them to 1/8" wood with too-long nails and screws resulted in them being able to shift.
In retrospect, I should have used 1/4".

Some external design, like a vinyl cut wrap or just paint, would definitely contribute to the final look.

The most significant problem, however, is the fact that the motor does not have enough force to overcome its static friction.
With no resistors, it spun unimpeded, but the dancer was just a blur. The 100 ohm resistor gives a nice speed, but it must be nudged to start.
My preferred speed would be much slower, but this motor really cannot go any slower without just stopping.

Also, the batteries cannot be replaced without yanking out the block and breaking the wiring in the back.
This should not be an issue, because I will probably revisit and overhaul this design before they die.
